Purpose
==================================================
The purpose of this BrainLift is to establish a strategy to define how AI can be
used in collaboration with learning science in STEM education products. One
important feature is that the app should be able to teach and instruct without any
AI features. It should present some truth to the students (in any domain where the
answer is computable) in the form of direct instruction, enabling them to
establish a factbase to reason upon. The use of AI should only be introduced where
learning science suggests that normally a human tutor would be valuable. CircuitLab
tests this learning model by establishing an app that teaches introductory circuits
to high school students taking AP Electromagnetic Physics by letting them manipulate
live circuits (keeping intrinsic motivation and interest up through discovery) and
including AI to generate extra practice, hints, and explanations tailored to the
user's mistakes.


==================================================
In Scope
==================================================
Usage of AI: This BrainLift is all about how to responsibly use AI to teach students
subjects without allowing for cognitive offload, while still leveraging the various
benefits of AI in order to make the learning process smoother, quicker, and more
personalized.

Inclusion of Learning Science: The app should work without AI using learning science
principles. Additionally, the inclusion of AI should be based on learning science in
order to make sure that AI is used responsibly.

Adaptive Learning: Learning Science says that problems need to be in a specific range
of difficulty in order to encourage learning. If they are too easy, then the user
simply will not gain anything from solving them, and if they are too difficult, then
the user will not be able to solve them at all, and thus will not gain anything and
may also lose motivation. As such, apps should be designed to keep problems in the
range between these two extremes, which AI can be extremely effective in doing, when
used correctly.


==================================================
Out of Scope
==================================================
The curriculum of the app: These insights and the discoveries made in this BrainLift
can be applied to a wide variety of topics and curriculums and are not specific to
any certain focus.

AI Chatbot Tutors: This BrainLift focuses less on simply including AI Chatbots as
tutors with back and forth conversations and more on small hints, or specific
explanations generated by AI tutors, and generating extra practice problems using AI.
AI Chatbot tutors must be very carefully designed in order to ensure that they
encourage learning rather than cognitive offload, and this BrainLift doesn't focus on
how they should be used.

Teaching non-STEM subjects: This BrainLift focuses heavily on fields where a very
direct, verifiable answer is present and focuses less on topics where multiple
interpretations can all be correct.


==================================================
DOK 4: Spiky Points of View (SPOVs)
==================================================
SPOV 1 - Using AI is the best way to generate an actual adaptive learning path.
Textbooks with problems that slowly increase in difficulty are not one-fits-all as
they've been marketed to be and can easily be too quickly increasing in difficulty
for certain students and too slowly increasing in difficulty for others. Using AI to
personalize learning and the questions given per student is a much more effective way
to generate an adaptive learning path.

SPOV 2 - AI's used in education must be very strictly overlooked. Even in scenarios
where you aren't simply using an AI Chatbot, every definition needs to be very
specific in order to make an effective app. For example, when telling AI to generate
harder questions, you need to specify that "harder" must mean *structurally* harder,
not *verbally* harder. Left undefined, an AI optimizes the cheapest proxy — longer
sentences — and calls it rigor.

SPOV 3 - AI in an education product must be *load-bearing-free*. If pulling the API
key degrades the product, the core app never actually taught, and is thus
ineffective.

Experts
==================================================

Daniel Willingham
- Who: Cognitive psychologist, Professor at the University of Virginia; author of
  "Why Don't Students Like School?"
- Focus: How memory and attention actually work; why factual knowledge precedes
  skill; "memory is the residue of thought."
- Why Follow: The foundational case that learning lives in the learner's own
  effortful thinking. Grounds SPOV 3 — if AI does the thinking, no learning happens,
  so AI must stay additive over a self-sufficient instructional core.
- Where: https://www.danielwillingham.com

John Sweller & Paul Kirschner
- Who: Sweller — originator of Cognitive Load Theory (UNSW). Kirschner —
  educational psychologist (Open University of the Netherlands), co-author of
  "How Learning Happens."
- Focus: Working-memory limits; the worked-example effect; why explicit, guided
  instruction beats minimal-guidance discovery for novices.
- Why Follow: CLT is the mechanism behind SPOV 2. "Harder = wordier" inflates
  *extraneous* load and teaches reading comprehension, not circuits; real rigor
  raises *germane* load through structural complexity.
- Where: Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006) —
  https://research.ou.nl/ws/files/1015152/Why%20minimal%20guidance%20during%20instruction%20does%20not%20work.pdf

Lev Vygotsky
- Who: Soviet psychologist; originator of the Zone of Proximal Development.
- Focus: The band between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with
  guidance; learning happens at the edge of current ability.
- Why Follow: The theoretical core of SPOV 1. A fixed textbook ramp cannot stay in
  every learner's ZPD at once; per-student AI difficulty targeting can.
- Where: Vygotsky (1978), "Mind in Society" —
  https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674576292

Benjamin Bloom & Barak Rosenshine
- Who: Bloom — educational psychologist; "2 Sigma Problem" and mastery learning.
  Rosenshine — educational psychologist; "Principles of Instruction."
- Focus: Bloom — tutoring + mastery feedback moves the average student ~2 standard
  deviations. Rosenshine — ten instructional principles, including obtaining a
  high (~80%) success rate during guided practice.
- Why Follow: Together they define the *target* of an adaptive path (SPOV 1): keep
  the learner near an ~80% success rate with error-specific corrective feedback —
  exactly the band AI can hold a student in problem-by-problem.
- Where: Rosenshine (2012) — https://www.aft.org/sites/default/files/Rosenshine.pdf ;
  Bloom (1984) — https://web.mit.edu/5.95/readings/bloom-two-sigma.pdf

Henry Roediger & Robert Bjork
- Who: Memory researchers (Washington University in St. Louis; UCLA); the
  retrieval-practice and "desirable difficulties" traditions; Roediger co-authored
  "Make It Stick."
- Focus: Testing as a learning event (not just measurement); spacing; conditions
  that slow apparent learning but boost durable retention.
- Why Follow: Why an endless, right-difficulty practice stream (SPOV 1) is higher
  leverage than more exposition, and why productive struggle — not ease — is the
  goal.
- Where: Roediger & Karpicke (2006) —
  https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x

Richard Hake & Carl Wieman
- Who: Hake — physicist behind the landmark interactive-engagement study. Wieman —
  Nobel laureate physicist, founder of PhET Interactive Simulations.
- Focus: Active/interactive engagement in physics; manipulable simulations; "doing"
  beats "watching" for conceptual gain.
- Why Follow: The empirical spine of CircuitLab's AI-free core (SPOV 3) and the
  ancestor of its live, manipulable circuit visuals.
- Where: PhET — https://phet.colorado.edu ; Hake (1998) —
  https://doi.org/10.1119/1.18809

Simon Willison
- Who: Co-creator of Django; practitioner-writer on applied LLM engineering.
- Focus: Treating models as untrusted components; grounding; verifying fact-bearing
  output; the unreliability of LLM-authored facts.
- Why Follow: The engineering articulation of SPOV 2 — specify tightly, ground in
  structured state, and verify every output rather than trusting the model.
- Where: https://simonwillison.net/tags/llms/

DOK 3: Insights
==================================================
- Supports SPOV 1: A single difficulty ramp fits no one exactly — too slow for some,
  too fast for others. The productive band is measurable (~80% success, Rosenshine;
  desirable difficulty, Bjork; the zone of proximal development, Vygotsky), so an AI
  escalator that raises difficulty on a clean solve and eases after a miss can hold
  each learner at their own edge. This vertical personalization (the right next rep)
  beats reordering the path.
- Supports SPOV 2: An undefined "make it harder" prompt optimizes the cheapest proxy
  — longer sentences — inflating extraneous load instead of building schemas. And
  because transformers fail at multi-step arithmetic, the model must only produce
  language while a deterministic engine produces and verifies every number: propose
  with the model, dispose with the engine.
- Supports SPOV 3: Learning is the residue of the learner's own thought, so the
  AI-free core must build the factbase through direct instruction and hands-on
  manipulation. "Remove the AI" is the truest test — if teaching stops when the key
  is pulled, the model was doing the work and the core never taught.

DOK 2: Knowledge Tree (with DOK 1 facts)
==================================================
Cognitive science of learning (supports SPOV 2, SPOV 3)
- Willingham, "Why Don't Students Like School?": memory is the residue of thought;
  working memory is limited and freed by knowledge -> learning lives in the learner's
  effort, so AI stays additive. https://www.danielwillingham.com
- Sweller CLT + Kirschner/Sweller/Clark (2006): working memory is tiny; worked
  examples cut extraneous load; guided beats discovery for novices -> "harder =
  wordier" raises the wrong load.
  https://research.ou.nl/ws/files/1015152/Why%20minimal%20guidance%20during%20instruction%20does%20not%20work.pdf
- Roediger & Karpicke (2006) + Bjork desirable difficulties + Dunlosky (2013):
  testing and spacing beat rereading; dosed struggle builds durable memory ->
  endless adaptive practice is the highest-leverage AI feature.
  https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/pdf/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x

Adaptive difficulty and mastery (supports SPOV 1)
- Vygotsky (ZPD): teach just above independent ability; a fixed ramp cannot fit every
  learner, AI targeting can. Rosenshine (2012): aim for ~80% success
  (https://www.aft.org/sites/default/files/Rosenshine.pdf). Bloom (1984): tutoring +
  corrective feedback ~ 2 sigma (https://web.mit.edu/5.95/readings/bloom-two-sigma.pdf).

Active-learning core, AI-free (supports SPOV 3)
- Hake (1998, 6,542 students): interactive engagement ~2x the FCI gain of lecture.
  Freeman (2014, PNAS): active learning cut failure rates 34% -> 22%
  (https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1319030111). OpenStax Univ. Physics Vol. 2: the
  CC-licensed ground truth for every formula (https://openstax.org/books/university-physics-volume-2).

LLMs as untrusted reasoners (supports SPOV 2)
- Dziri (2023) "Faith and Fate": transformer accuracy collapses on multi-step
  arithmetic (https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18654). Gao (2022) "PAL": offload computation
  to a deterministic runtime. Lightman (2023) "Let's Verify": step-level verification
  beats outcome trust -> model frames, engine computes and verifies.

CircuitLab implementation (primary source — this codebase)
- Deterministic engines (types.ts, network.ts) own and verify every number; the AI
  backbone (src/lib/ai/*) follows structured state -> engine truth -> model language
  -> guardrail re-check, with an AI-off fallback for every feature, so AI is additive,
  never load-bearing.

Phase 3: Learning Science Layered In (primary source — this codebase)
==================================================
Phase 3 adds techniques to make recall durable, not just to get a learner through a
lesson once. Each is implemented against the same deterministic substrate (so it all
works with AI off) and is made *visible*, because an invisible learning gain is one a
learner won't trust or act on.

Why these fit circuits: the subject decomposes into a small set of discrete, recurring
concepts (Ohm's law, series-add, parallel-reciprocal, equivalent resistance, power,
multi-source). That is exactly the shape spaced repetition needs as schedulable
"items," and the engine + practice generator can mint unlimited fresh problems per
concept — so a review is genuine retrieval (solve a new problem from memory), not the
memorization of one flashcard's answer.

1. Spaced repetition (Roediger & Karpicke; Bjork desirable difficulties)
   - src/lib/review.ts — a per-concept Leitner model (boxes 0..5). A clean recall
     promotes a concept to a longer interval (1, 3, 7, 14, 30 days); a miss demotes it
     two boxes so it resurfaces sooner. Concepts are seeded when their lesson is first
     mastered (AuthContext.completeStep), persisted in UserState.review (localStorage +
     a tolerant Supabase review_state column).
   - "Strength" decays once a concept is overdue, so neglect is visible rather than
     silent.

2. Retrieval practice (Roediger & Karpicke; "memory is the residue of thought",
   Willingham)
   - The Daily Review (src/components/review/ReviewSession.tsx, /review) asks the
     learner to *produce* an answer to a freshly generated problem for each due concept
     — recall, not recognition. No passive re-reading; every item is a test.

3. Interleaving (Bjork)
   - The review queue mixes due concepts (Fisher–Yates shuffle) instead of blocking by
     type, forcing the learner to *choose* the right method (series vs parallel vs
     power) rather than reuse the last rule — the classic circuits failure mode.

4. Scaffolding & desirable difficulty (Vygotsky ZPD; Rosenshine ~80% success)
   - Per-lesson "Practice more" still escalates difficulty on clean solves and eases
     after a miss (PracticeSession). The Daily Review pitches each problem at a
     difficulty scaled to the concept's current box (review.ts reviewDifficulty),
     fading support as strength grows while staying inside maxLevelForTopic.

5. Mastery learning, made legible (Bloom)
   - Unlock already gates on masteryScore >= masteryThreshold (storage.unlockNextLesson)
     with a "Retry Mastery Check" loop. Phase 3 makes the signal durable and visible:
     the course page shows per-concept retention % and a "review due" cue, and a
     retention dashboard (RetentionPanel) surfaces overall retention and how many
     concepts are due — so "mastered" is shown as a state that must be maintained, not
     a one-time checkbox.

6. Immediate, explanatory feedback (carried from Phase 1/2)
   - Wrong answers still get an engine-grounded diagnosis tuned to what the learner
     actually did (diagnose.ts), with all computed values redacted so feedback teaches
     the next step without leaking the answer.

Showing the effect: the RetentionPanel and per-lesson cards visualize strength bars,
due counts, and accuracy over time — the observable output of the spaced-repetition
model, readable straight from the deterministic memory state with no AI involved.
